# Wellbeing Signals: A Self-Reference

## What this document is

A lookup-style reference for noticing what's happening with yourself. Organized by what you might be experiencing, not by diagnostic category, because in the moment you usually have a feeling or a behavior — not a diagnosis — and you want to know what it might mean.

Each entry has three parts: the signal, what it could indicate, and a question that might help you sort it out. The questions are deliberately recognition-shaped (pick between options, yes/no) rather than open-ended, because those are easier to answer when capacity is low.

This is not a diagnostic tool. It's a personal reference for someone who already lives with ADHD, depression, and panic-around-leaving-the-house, and wants help noticing patterns. If something here surprises you about yourself, take it as a prompt to look more carefully, not as ground truth.

## Mood and feeling signals

### "I feel low"
- **Could be:** Ordinary bad mood; ADHD emotional dysregulation (often shorter-lived, often triggered); depressive episode (more sustained, less tied to a clear trigger); aftermath of a panic episode.
- **Sorting question:** Has this lasted most of the day for several days, or did it spike and pass within hours?

### "I'm irritable, snappy, things are bothering me more than usual"
- **Could be:** ADHD emotional dysregulation; early sign of a depressive episode (irritability is often the first to shift, before sadness); poor sleep; hunger; medication off-schedule; impending panic.
- **Sorting question:** When did you last eat a real meal and sleep a real night?

### "I feel nothing — not sad, just flat"
- **Could be:** Anhedonia, which is a depression core feature; emotional numbing after panic; ADHD emotional dysregulation in a low-arousal direction; dissociation under chronic stress.
- **Sorting question:** Have things you usually enjoy stopped feeling enjoyable, or are you just not interested in starting them?
- **Why the distinction matters:** "Stopped feeling enjoyable" leans depression; "not interested in starting" leans ADHD/executive function. Often it's both, but they need different kinds of support.

### "I feel hopeless about the future"
- **Could be:** A depressive cognition (specifically the kind that, alongside ADHD, signals real depression rather than ADHD baseline frustration); aftermath of a setback that hasn't yet been processed; ongoing chronic stress without breaks.
- **Sorting question:** Is this about a specific thing in the future, or about the future as a whole?
- **If it's about the future as a whole:** This is one of the warning signs to take seriously. Tell someone.

### "I feel like a burden / like nothing I do matters"
- **Could be:** Cognitive distortion in depression. Particularly important to notice because these specific thoughts are correlated with elevated suicide risk.
- **What to do:** Treat this as a signal to reach out, not as a fact about you. The thought is information about your current brain state, not about your worth.

## Body and energy signals

### "I'm exhausted but it's not a normal tired"
- **Could be:** Depression fatigue (often worst in the morning, doesn't improve with rest); post-panic exhaustion; ADHD burnout from sustained masking; physical illness; medication side effect.
- **Sorting question:** Does the tiredness improve at all if you do something, or does it stay flat regardless?
- **Why this matters:** Depression fatigue is often unresponsive to rest. ADHD burnout often improves with novelty or interest. Different responses suggest different supports.

### "I can't sleep / I'm sleeping too much"
- **Could be:** Both directions are depression markers; ADHD often produces delayed sleep phase; panic can drive insomnia from anticipatory anxiety.
- **Sorting question:** Is the problem getting to sleep, staying asleep, or wanting to get out of bed once awake?

### "I haven't eaten / I'm eating too much"
- **Could be:** Depression appetite changes; ADHD interoception failure (genuinely not noticing hunger); stress eating; medication effect.
- **Sorting question:** Are you not hungry, or are you hungry but it feels like too much effort to do something about it?
- **Why this matters:** "Not hungry" is more often a body signal. "Too much effort" is more often executive dysfunction and may need a different intervention (someone bringing you food vs. checking what sounds appealing).

### "My body feels heavy / I can't move"
- **Could be:** Depression psychomotor retardation; freeze response after panic or near-panic; sensory overload aftermath.
- **Sorting question:** If a fire alarm went off, would you move? (Not asking you to test this. Just to gauge whether it's "physically can't" or "the cost of moving feels enormous.")

## Doing-things signals

### "I can't start the thing"
- **Could be:** ADHD task initiation (the wall of awful); depression motivational deficit; avoidance because the task triggers panic; perfectionism; the task is genuinely too big without breakdown.
- **Sorting question:** Does the task feel boring, scary, or impossibly heavy?
- **Why this matters:** Boring → ADHD support (dopamine engineering). Scary → exposure/safety. Heavy → depression support (micro-step breakdown). Treatments differ.

### "I keep forgetting things I should remember"
- **Could be:** ADHD working memory; depression cognitive impairment ("brain fog" is real and measurable); sleep deprivation; chronic stress.
- **Sorting question:** Is it everything, or specifically things connected to particular feelings?
- **Why this matters:** Forgetting connected to a topic ("I keep forgetting to schedule that appointment") often has an avoidance component. Forgetting everything is more likely cognitive load.

### "I'm doing too much, can't stop"
- **Could be:** ADHD hyperfocus (can be productive or harmful); manic-spectrum elevation; avoidance of something else by burying yourself in this; trauma response.
- **Sorting question:** When did you last eat, drink, use the bathroom, or move from this position?
- **Why this matters:** Hyperfocus that's missed basic body needs is no longer productive — it's harmful. Familiar's "intelligent disobedience" should kick in here.

### "I keep starting things and abandoning them"
- **Could be:** ADHD baseline; depression-driven loss of follow-through; trying to do too many things at once because you're scared to commit to one.
- **Note:** This one is rarely a warning sign by itself for someone with ADHD. The question is whether it's worse than your usual.

## Outside-the-house signals

### "Going outside feels harder than usual"
- **Could be:** Agoraphobia worsening; depression-driven withdrawal; current stressor making the world feel more dangerous; physical illness reducing capacity.
- **Sorting question:** When you imagine going outside, what's the feeling — fear, exhaustion, or "no point"?
- **Why this matters:** Each of those leans toward a different cluster (fear → exposure; exhaustion → executive support; "no point" → depression).

### "My safe zone is shrinking"
- **What this means:** Places that used to be tolerable are no longer. This is a key agoraphobia warning sign, separate from any specific panic episode.
- **What to do:** Note this clearly. Shrinking safe zones tend to keep shrinking unless something pushes back. Don't necessarily push back today, but don't ignore it.

### "I had a panic attack / nearly had one"
- **Important after:** The hours and days following a panic episode are when avoidance gets cemented. The instinct will be to never go back to wherever it happened.
- **Sorting question:** Are you avoiding because it's genuinely unsafe, or because the place is now associated with the panic?

## When to treat something as serious

These are signals where the right response is to reach out to a human, not just notice and continue:

- Thoughts of suicide, even passing ones, even ones that feel philosophical
- Statements about being a burden, the world being better without you, or "giving up"
- Sudden calm after a period of intense distress (this can mean a decision has been reached, and not always a safe one)
- Researching methods of self-harm
- Withdrawal from your support network specifically because you don't want them to "have to deal with you"
- Any of the above happening to a friend rather than yourself

If you're noticing these in yourself: this is exactly when reaching out is hardest and most necessary. Familiar should be helping you do it. If Familiar is not yet built or is not enough, the human options remain: someone you trust, a crisis line, an emergency service.

## A note on baselines

Most of the signals in this document need to be read against *your* baseline, not a generic one. Some examples:

- "Forgetting things" is normal-level for ADHD; the question is whether it's *worse than your usual ADHD level*.
- "Trouble starting tasks" is the same.
- "Disrupted sleep" might be your baseline if you have ADHD-typical delayed sleep phase. The question is whether it's worsened.
- "Some social withdrawal" might be how you recover from socializing. Withdrawal that exceeds what you need to recover is the signal.

Tracking over time is more useful than any single reading. A change from your baseline matters more than where the absolute value lands relative to a generic population.

## Caveat

This document is built from clinical research literature on ADHD, depression, and panic/agoraphobia, plus the specific concern that comorbid presentations differ from either condition alone. It is informational, not diagnostic. The instruments referenced (PHQ-9, WHO-5, ASRS, PDSS) exist and are validated, but using them for self-tracking is different from using them clinically — a real assessment by a real human is its own thing and not replaceable.

The clinical literature is heavily weighted toward Western, employed, neurotypical-default populations, and most of the screening tools were developed in those contexts. They tend to over-pathologize neurodivergent baselines and miss things that are normal for ADHD. Read everything here with that in mind.
